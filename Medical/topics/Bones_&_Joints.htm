Bones & Joints

Classification of bones

  • According to shape

Long bones

elongated shaft (diaphysis), two expanded ends (epiphyses), 3 surfaces, 3 borders, a central medullary cavity, a nutrient foramen directed away from the growing end

  1. typical long bones: humerus, radius, ulna, femur, tibia and fibula
  2. miniature long bones: one epiphysis, eg: metacarpals, metatarsals and phalanges
  3. modified long bones: no medullary cavity like clavicle
 
Short bones named according to shape tarsals, carpals   
Flat bones resembles plates, form boundaries of body cavities bones in the vault of the skull, ribs, sternum, scapula 
Irregular bones irregular shapes vertebra, hip bone, bones in the base of the skull   
Pneumatic bones skull bones having air filled cavities called air sinuses, skull light in weight, resonance of voice, act as a/c for the inspired air maxilla, sphenoid, ethmoid  
Sesamoid bones seedlike, small nodules of bone, in tendons of certain muscles, resist pressure, minimise friction, maintain the local circulation patella, pisiform 

  • Developmental Classification

Membrane bones ossification center appear in the mesenchymatous model without forming the cartilaginous model, intramembranous/mesenchymal ossification bones of the vault of skull 
Cartilaginous bones first a cartilaginous model of bone is formed and then ossi centre appear within the cartilaginous model and replace it with bone bones of limbs, vertebral column, thoracic cage  
Membrano-cartilaginous bones ossify partly in membrane and partly in cartilage clavicle, mandible  

  • Structural Classification

Macroscopically

Compact bone

deep to the periosteum, looks like ivory with naked eye, made up of haversian systems and canals, red bone marrow which become yellow after puberty bc of fat

Cancellous/Spongy bone  looks like sponge, bony lamellae enclosing narrow spaces containing the bone marrow tissue (red)

¥ Wolff's law: bone formation is directly proportional to stress and strain, both tensile and compressive forces can stimulate bone formation in proper conditions

Microscopically

Lamellar bone  most mature human bones (compact & spongy) are composed of thin plates of bony tissue called lamellae, arranged in piles in a cancellous bone and in concentric cylinders (Haversian system) in a compact bone 
Woven Bone  in fetal bone, fracture repair and in cancer of bone 
Fibrous bone  in young foetal bones 
Dentine and Cement  in teeth 

Ossification

  1. The primary center of ossification usually appears before birth and forms the main part of the bone. In long bone it forms the diaphysis.
  2. The secondary centers of ossification usually appear after birth and form small parts of the bone called epiphyses.
  3. Law of ossification states that secondary centres of ossification which appear first are last to unite.
  4. The growing end of the long bone is that end of the bone whose epiphysis fuses with the diaphysis (shaft) later than the other end. It lies opposite to the direction of nutrient foramen.

Long Bone

  • Adult long bone        
    1. Shaft:
      • Periosteum: outer fibrous layer and an inner cellular layer osteogenic in nature, united to the underlying bone by Sharpey's fibres,  rich nerve supply, nourishes the bone, helps in growth and repair
      • Cortex: compact bone, gives strength
      • Medullary cavity: filled with red or yellow bone marrow
    2. Ends: cancellous bone, covered with hyaline (articular) cartilage

  • Young long bone 
    • Epiphysis:
      1. Pressure: articular, takes part in transmission of the weight, eg: head of femur; lower end of radius 
      2. Traction: nonarticular, provides attachment to tendons which exert a traction on the epiphysis, eg: trochanters of femur, tubercles of humerus
      3. Atavistic: independent bone in lower animal which becomes fused in man, eg: coracoid process of scapula 
      4. Aberrant: not always present, eg: head of the first metacarpal and at the base of other metacarpal bones         
    • Diaphysis: elongated shaft, ossifies from a primary centre
    • Metaphysis: epiphysial ends of a diaphysis, zone of active growth
    • Epiphysial Plate of Cartilage: separates epiphysis from metaphysis, lengthwise growth, after fusion bone can no longer grow in length, nurished by both the epiphysial and metaphysial arteries
  • Blood Supply

    • Nutrient artery: enters thru nutrient foramen (elbow-go, knee-flee) into cortex and divides into ascending and descending branches in the medullary cavity, terminate in the adult metaphysis by anastomosing with the epiphysial, metaphysial and periosteal arteries, supplies medullary cavity and inner 2/3 cortex
    • Periosteal arteries: beneath the muscular attachement and nourished by the muscular vessels supplying muscles, supplies outer 1/3 cortex
    • Epiphysial arteries: derived from vessels supplying the articular capsule of the joint
    • Metaphysial arteries: before epiphysial fusion the metaphysis is richly supplied with blood through end arteries forming 'hair-pin' bends, after the fusion anastomosis b/w epi and meta arteries




Joints

  • Synarthroses: immovable
    •  Fibrous joints

Sutures  peculiar to skull 
Syndesmosis  bones connected by interosseous ligament, eg: inferior tibiofibular joint 
Gomphosis  root of the tooth in its bony socket 

  • Amphiarthrosis: slightly movable
    • Cartilaginous joints

Primary cartilaginous/Synchondrosis  united by plate of hyaline cartilage, temporary, replaced by bone (synostosis), eg: epiphysial plate,  costochondral joints, first chondrosternal joint 
Secondary cartilaginous/Symphysis  articular surfaces covered by hyaline cartilage and united by disc of fibrocartilage, permanent eg: symphysis pubis, intervertebral joints 

  • Diarthrosis: freely movable
    • Synovial joints
      • Plane/Gliding Joint: flat articular surfaces, eg: inter[carpal, tarsal, metacrapal, metatarsal], chondrosternal (except first)
      • Uniaxial joints

Hinge joint  pulley-shaped, movement around  a transverse axis, eg: elbow, ankle, interphalangeal joints 
Pivot joint  one forms central pivot  while the other is shaped like a ring, vertical axis, eg: atlanto-axial, superior and inferior radio-ulnar joints  

      • Biaxial joints

Condylar/Bicondylar joint  convex male with reciprocally concave female, transverse axis and partly in vertical, eg: knee, temporomandibular joint 
Ellipsoid joint  oval convex male into elliptical concave female, free movement in both axis, eg: wrist, atlanto-occipital joints

      • Multiaxial joints

Saddle joint  reciprocally concavoconvex, eg: first carpometacarpal, sternoclavicular joint 
Ball and socket joint  globular head male into cup-shaped female, eg: shoulder, hip joint

Synovial Joint 

  • Articular surfaces are covered with hyaline (articular) cartilage.
  • Between the articular surfaces there is a joint cavity filled with synovial fluid which lubricates the joint and nourishes the articular cartilage.
  • The joint is surrounded by an articular capsule which is made up of a fibrous capsule lined by synovial membrane.
  • The synovial membrane lines whole of the interior of the joint, except for the articular surfaces covered by hyaline cartilage
  • Capsule and ligaments possess a rich nerve supply, which makes them acutely sensitive to pain.
  • The synovial membrane has a poor nerve supply and is relatively insensitive to pain.
  • The articular cartilage is non-nervous and totally insensitive.
  • Hilton's law states that a motor nerve to the muscle acting on joint tends to give a branch to that joint (capsule) and another branch to the skin covering the joint.
